K-Means Manual Exercise
Dataset Explanation

This exercise uses a small synthetic dataset representing students' weekly study hours and their exam scores. The goal is to manually apply the K-Means algorithm to find natural student groupings.

We use K = 3 clusters (Low, Medium, High performers) and 2 features:

  • X: Study hours per week
  • Y: Exam score (out of 100)

Iteration 1 – Distance Calculations

For each point, we calculate the Euclidean distance to each centroid:

d(P, C) = √( (Xp − Xc)² + (Yp − Yc)² )

Example – Student S1 (2, 35):

d(S1, C1) = √((2−2)²+(35−35)²) = 0.00
d(S1, C2) = √((2−6)²+(35−60)²) = √(16+625) = √641 = 25.32
d(S1, C3) = √((2−10)²+(35−88)²) = √(64+2809) = √2873 = 53.60

→ S1 is assigned to C1 (lowest distance = 0.00)

Results Interpretation

After 2 iterations, K-Means successfully grouped students into 3 meaningful clusters:

  • Cluster 1 – Low Performers: Students S1, S2, S3 — 2 to 4 study hours, scores 35–45
  • Cluster 2 – Medium Performers: Students S4, S5, S6 — 5 to 7 study hours, scores 55–65
  • Cluster 3 – High Performers: Students S7, S8, S9, S10 — 8 to 11 study hours, scores 75–95

The exercise confirms a strong positive relationship between study hours and exam performance. K-Means was able to detect this structure without any label — demonstrating the power of unsupervised learning.

The algorithm converged in just 2 iterations because the data is well-separated into distinct linear groups. In more complex real-world datasets, more iterations and multiple runs are typically required.
